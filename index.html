States With Greater Incidences of Poverty Also Show Greater Lack of Healthcare

Data obtained from the U.S. Census Bureau and Behavioral Risk Factor Surveillance System show that a positive correlation exists between states' poverty rate and their percentage of population lacking healthcare. This comes as no surprise - it makes sense logically that the greater the population you have living below the poverty line, the more people you'll have that can't afford medical care and health insurance.

As seen in the above scatter plot, Texas has the greatest number of people lacking healthcare at over 24% of the population with a poverty rate approaching 18%. Other states struggling most include Georgia, Louisiana, New Mexico, and Mississippi. A couple of states, West Virginia and Kentucky, do not show as great a percentage lacking healthcare despite having poverty rates as high or greater than the previously mentioned struggling states. This could be due to lower state populations but more in depth research is needed to account for these differences.

The fact remains that the have-nots in our society also go without proper medical care, a grim reality that we must all work together to fight so that no individual goes without the care they need. If everyone were to have regular exams and proactive care, it could ultimately ease the burden on our medical system by keeping individuals healthy - avoiding extreme and costly procedures for ailments that could have been prevented.
